# Problem-Space Divergence Method

## Dimension

Use "dimension" primarily as degree of freedom: a knob whose variation changes the problem. Also recognize it as a modeling choice: naming an axis is a commitment about what variation matters. Avoid treating every mentioned topic as a dimension.

## Collapse Discipline

Expand before collapsing. A single pass usually picks the most salient axis, not the load-bearing one.

For each candidate axis pair, ask:

- Can one axis be derived from the other by changing only frequency?
- Can one axis be derived from the other by changing vocabulary?
- Can one axis be derived from the other by changing framing?
- Do they co-vary perfectly in the situations that matter?

If yes, fuse them. Fusing means they are one axis; it does not mean either concern is unimportant.

## Residue

The residue is whatever remains orthogonal to the collapsed axes. It is often human judgment, goals, taste, trust, accountability, or irreducible choice. Do not collapse it away to make the map tidy.

## Stress Tests

- **Buried contradiction:** Does the solution require what the problem claims is impossible?
- **Hidden gray zone:** Does a clean binary hide the dangerous middle case?
- **Wrong verb:** Does a static verb hide a continuous cost?

Revise the collapse until the stress test no longer breaks it.
